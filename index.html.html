00 — Introduction

About This Project

The National Basketball Association (NBA) provides one of the most data-intensive leagues in professional sports. Each team in the league analyzes hundreds of performance measures for each of its players every game, giving rise to data sets that are perfect for statistical analysis. This project will use concepts from three main topics in probability and statistics for a sample of 450 players in the NBA during the 2023–24 season.

As was the case throughout the entire course, the three parts in this project follow a logical order: first, we will describe and visualize the data (Descriptive Statistics, Ch. 1); second, we will calculate probabilities of events occurring (Probability, Ch. 2); finally, we will compute confidence intervals for various measures (Confidence Intervals, Ch. 7).

Variables used include points per game (PPG), rebounds per game (RPG), assists per game (APG), shooting percentage (FG%), player efficiency rating (PER), player position, and games played. The data set corresponds to publicly available data on the NBA, as reported on Basketball-Reference.com.

Project Goal: Use real statistical methods — not just intuition — to answer meaningful questions about what separates average players from elite performers, and how position shapes statistical outcomes in the NBA.

Rather than examining all 450 individual player lines, we compute measures of location (where the data is centered) and variability (how spread out the data is) to paint a clear picture of the typical NBA player.

2.3 Conditional Probability

Conditional probability asks: given that we already know something about the player, how does that change the likelihood of another outcome? We use the formula:

P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)

2.5 Testing for Independence

Two events are independent if P(A | B) = P(A). We test whether being a Guard and being a scorer are independent:

P(≥20 PPG) = 0.140  |  P(≥20 PPG | Guard) = 0.235

Since 0.235 ≠ 0.140, the events are NOT independent. Being a Guard significantly increases the probability of being a 20+ PPG scorer.
Key Finding §2: Guards are 3.5× more likely to score 20+ PPG than Centers (23.5% vs. 6.7%), while Centers are 25× more likely to grab 10+ RPG than Guards (30.0% vs. 1.2%). Position and performance outcomes are not independent.
03 — Chapter 7.1–7.3

Section 3: Confidence Intervals

A confidence interval (CI) gives us a range of plausible values for a true population parameter based on sample data. Rather than saying "the average PPG is 12.6," a CI lets us say "we are 95% confident the true mean PPG for all NBA-caliber players falls between 11.99 and 13.21."

7.1 The Large-Sample CI Formula

Because our sample size (n = 450) is large, we apply the large-sample CI formula from section 7.2:

x̄ ± z* (s / √n)
where z* = 1.645 (90%), 1.960 (95%), or 2.576 (99%)

7.2 CI for Mean PPG — Full Sample

Using x̄ = 12.6, s = 6.50, n = 450, the standard error = 6.50 / √450 = 0.3065:

Confidence Levelz*Margin of ErrorLower BoundUpper Bound
90%1.645±0.50412.09613.104
95%1.960±0.60111.99913.201
99%2.576±0.79011.81013.390

As the confidence level increases, the margin of error widens — this is the classic trade-off: more certainty requires a wider interval.

04 — Summary & Findings

Summary & Findings

This project used a dataset of 450 NBA players from the 2023–2024 season to apply three fundamental areas of probability and statistics. Each part built upon the previous one: confidence intervals verified that observed differences represent actual population-level patterns, probability defined position-based likelihoods, and descriptive statistics showed the data's structure.

01
Descriptive Statistics
PPG has s = 6.50 and is right-skewed (mean 12.6, median 10.4). The average PPG difference between guards and centers is 5.2. While great scorers form a long right tail, the majority of players score between 5 and 15 points every game.
02
Probability
Position and scoring are not independent. A Guard is 3.5× more likely to score 20+ PPG than a Center. Centers dominate rebounding probability (30% chance of 10+ RPG vs. 1.2% for Guards).
03
Confidence Intervals
At 95% confidence, the true mean PPG for Guards [17.34, 19.46] does not overlap with Centers [12.10, 14.30], statistically confirming the position–scoring relationship at the population level.

Real-World Implications

These findings have direct applications in sports analytics. An NBA front office evaluating trades or draft picks can use conditional probabilities to assess how a player's position predicts their likely statistical contribution. Confidence intervals help teams determine whether a player's performance in a small sample is likely to persist over a full season.

The same statistical toolkit — descriptive summaries, probability, and confidence intervals — applies directly to careers in data science, finance, healthcare analytics, and engineering quality control, demonstrating the broad relevance of this course material beyond basketball.
